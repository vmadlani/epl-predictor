What is the Dixon-Coles model?

Dixon-Coles is a statistical model for predicting football match scores, published by Mark Dixon and Stuart Coles in 1997. Each team has two numbers — an attack rating and a defence rating — and the model uses these to estimate expected goals for each side in a given match.

The key refinement over basic Poisson regression is a small correction for low-scoring outcomes: matches ending 0-0, 1-0, 0-1, or 1-1 occur at slightly different rates than a naive Poisson model would predict. Correcting for this meaningfully improves accuracy on the outcomes that matter most.

This is the same family of models used by FiveThirtyEight for their club soccer forecasts. The difference is that this model is trained exclusively on Premier League data, which captures league-specific home advantage and scoring patterns more precisely.

What data does it use?

Five seasons of Premier League results: 2021-22 through 2025-26. Not all matches are treated equally — recent results carry more weight through time-decay weighting. A match from last month is roughly three times more informative than one from two seasons ago.

Home advantage is modelled as a single constant. The current estimate is approximately 0.25 log-goals, meaning home teams score around 28% more on average than they would away.

How are match probabilities generated?

For each fixture, the model:

  • Calculates expected goals for home: exp(attack_home + defence_away + home_adv)
  • Calculates expected goals for away: exp(attack_away + defence_home)
  • Builds a full score probability matrix — every scoreline from 0-0 to 8-8
  • Applies the Dixon-Coles correction for 0-0, 1-0, 0-1, and 1-1
  • Sums across rows and columns to produce Home Win%, Draw%, Away Win%

The score matrix is why the site can show probability for every specific scoreline — the expanded fixture cards show this matrix directly, darker cells indicating higher probability.

How are season projections made?

Monte Carlo simulation over 10,000 runs. For each simulation, the model samples a random result for every remaining fixture in proportion to its predicted probabilities, then tallies final points. Aggregating across all 10,000 runs produces the P10/median/P90 ranges, title probability, top-4 probability, and relegation probability shown in the table.

The team page shows this as a histogram — the spread of outcomes makes uncertainty visible rather than hiding it behind a single number.

How accurate is it?

Validation uses Ranked Probability Score (RPS) — a metric that rewards probability distributions that put mass near the true outcome, not just the most likely result. Compared against Bet365 historical closing odds, the model has beaten Bet365 on RPS in both 2024-25 and 2025-26. Closing odds already incorporate sharp bettor information, so beating them is a meaningful benchmark.

How often does it update?

After each gameweek. New match data is downloaded from football-data.co.uk, ratings are re-trained from scratch, and all projections are recalculated. The full pipeline takes approximately two minutes. The header shows which gameweek was last processed.

Limitations

The model does not account for: team news (injuries, suspensions), mid-season managerial changes, transfer window activity, or weather and pitch conditions. These factors will only show up in the ratings once they affect on-pitch results.

Despite this, the RPS track record suggests the model is a useful tool for reasoning about likely outcomes — not a crystal ball, but better than unaided intuition or implied bookmaker odds.
